Baby Food Allergies: What Every Dad Should Know

By Tom | February 2026 | 6 min read

food allergies are the part of weaning that terrifies new parents. and I get it. you're giving your baby something new and there's a small chance their body decides it's the enemy. that's genuinely scary.

but here's the good news: the research on this has moved a lot in the last decade, and the advice now is clearer than it's ever been. early introduction is protective, most reactions are mild, and there's a system for doing this that makes it manageable.

The big shift: introduce early, not late

for years, the advice was to delay common allergens. don't give peanuts until age 3. avoid eggs until 12 months. the thinking was that giving allergens later would reduce the risk of allergy.

turns out, that was backwards.

the LEAP trial (Learning Early About Peanut allergy, 2015) showed that introducing peanuts to high-risk babies between 4 and 11 months reduced peanut allergy by about 80%. the EAT study (2016) supported similar findings across multiple allergens.

current NHS and BSACI guidance: introduce common allergens from around 6 months of age (or whenever your baby starts solids). don't delay them. early exposure is protective.

The 14 major allergens

in the UK, these are the 14 allergens that must be declared on food labels. the top 8 are the ones most relevant for baby weaning:

Allergen How to introduce When
Peanuts Smooth peanut butter mixed into porridge From 6 months
Eggs Well-cooked scrambled egg From 6 months
Cow's milk In cooking (not as a drink until 12m) From 6 months
Wheat Toast fingers, pasta From 6 months
Soya Tofu pieces, soy in sauces From 6 months
Fish Cooked flaked white fish From 6 months
Sesame Tahini mixed into purees From 6 months
Tree nuts Nut butters (never whole nuts) From 6 months

The method: one at a time

the standard approach is simple:

  1. introduce one new allergen. give a small amount, ideally in the morning so you can watch for reactions during the day.
  2. wait 2-3 days. watch for any reaction. if nothing happens, that allergen is cleared.
  3. keep giving it regularly. this is the bit people forget. one-off exposure isn't enough. keep that food in the rotation, ideally weekly.
  4. introduce the next allergen. repeat the cycle.

at this pace, you'll have all 8 major allergens introduced within about a month. not bad.

What reactions look like

Mild reactions (most common)

  • redness or hives around the mouth or on the skin
  • mild swelling of lips or eyes
  • runny nose or sneezing
  • stomach upset, vomiting
  • itchy skin

these usually appear within minutes to a couple of hours. they're unpleasant but not dangerous. antihistamine (like Piriton, check with your pharmacist for the right dose) usually sorts it.

Severe reactions (rare but important)

call 999 immediately if you see:

- difficulty breathing or wheezing

- swelling of the tongue or throat

- baby going floppy or unresponsive

- persistent vomiting

this is anaphylaxis. it's very rare in babies but you need to know the signs. don't wait to see if it gets better.

Tracking matters

here's where it gets practical. when you're introducing 8 allergens over the course of a month, while also giving your baby normal food, and you're sleep-deprived and can't remember what day it is... you need to write things down.

I used a notebook at first. then I lost the notebook. then I used my phone notes. then I couldn't find the note. this is why I built the allergen tracker in DadBites.

the app logs what you've introduced, when, and whether there was any reaction. it tells you which allergens you haven't tried yet. and it reminds you to keep giving cleared allergens regularly (the "keep it in rotation" part that everyone forgets).

Family history and higher risk

if your baby has eczema, or there's a family history of food allergies, asthma, or eczema, your baby is at higher risk of developing a food allergy. this doesn't mean you should delay introduction. if anything, early introduction is even more important for these babies.

but talk to your GP or health visitor first. they might suggest introducing peanut earlier (from around 4 months in some cases) or doing it under medical supervision for the first time.

this article is informational, not medical advice. every baby is different. if you're unsure about anything, talk to your GP or health visitor. they're there for exactly this kind of question.
