When to Start Your Baby on Solids: A Dad's Guide

By Tom | February 2026 | 7 min read

nobody tells you how confusing weaning is until you're standing in Tesco staring at baby food pouches, wondering if your 5-month-old is "ready" or if you're about to do something terrible.

the NHS says around 6 months. your mum says she gave you rusks at 4 months and you turned out fine. the internet has 47 conflicting opinions. and your partner sent you a link to something called "baby-led weaning" which apparently means you just hand the baby a whole broccoli.

here's what I actually found useful when I went through this.

The official guidance (and why 6 months)

the World Health Organisation and the NHS both recommend exclusive breastfeeding or formula until around 6 months. then you start introducing solid foods alongside milk.

why 6 months specifically? a few reasons:

  • their digestive system is mature enough to handle non-milk foods
  • they can sit up with support and hold their head steady
  • they've mostly lost the tongue-thrust reflex (which pushes food out of their mouth)
  • they can coordinate their hands to grab things and bring them to their mouth

the key word is "around" 6 months. some babies are ready a bit before, some a bit after. the signs matter more than the exact date on the calendar.

Signs your baby is actually ready

look for all three of these together:

  1. they can sit up and hold their head steady. not propped up with cushions. actually sitting with support and controlling their head.
  2. they can coordinate eyes, hands, and mouth. they look at food, grab it, and bring it to their mouth. not randomly, intentionally.
  3. they can swallow food. if they push everything back out with their tongue, the tongue-thrust reflex hasn't gone yet. give it another week.

not signs of readiness: waking up more at night, chewing fists, wanting more milk. these are normal baby things that happen at any age. they don't mean your baby needs food.

the goal at the start isn't nutrition. they're still getting everything they need from milk. the goal is exploration, texture, taste, and learning to move food around their mouth.

Purees vs baby-led weaning

quick summary: purees means you spoon-feed smooth foods that gradually get lumpier. baby-led weaning (BLW) means you give them soft finger foods from the start and let them feed themselves.

the research says both approaches work fine. most parents end up doing a mix of both. BLW is brilliant for developing motor skills and letting the baby set the pace. purees are brilliant for getting food into a baby who's not great at self-feeding yet.

don't stress about picking the "right" method. pick whichever one means your baby actually eats something and you don't lose your mind.

The allergen question

this is the bit that panics most new parents. and the guidance has changed a lot in the last 10 years.

the current advice (as of 2024/2025, NHS and BSACI): introduce common allergens early and often. from around 6 months, not later. this includes:

  • peanuts (as smooth peanut butter, not whole nuts)
  • eggs (well cooked)
  • cow's milk (in cooking, not as a drink)
  • wheat (bread, pasta)
  • fish
  • sesame
  • soya
  • tree nuts (as butters or finely ground)

the LEAP study showed that introducing peanuts early (from 4-6 months) reduced peanut allergy by about 80%. that's not a typo. early introduction is protective, not risky.

introduce one new allergen at a time. wait a couple of days before the next one. that way, if there's a reaction, you know what caused it. most reactions are mild (rash, bit of swelling). severe reactions are rare, but know the signs and have your GP's number handy.

Common mistakes (that I made)

  • starting too cautiously. I gave my kid rice cereal for two weeks straight because I was scared of everything else. babies need variety. be braver than I was.
  • expecting them to eat a lot. at 6 months, "eating" means tasting, licking, throwing on the floor. that's normal. they're not replacing meals yet.
  • not offering enough. research says it can take 10-15 exposures before a baby accepts a new food. just because they spat out carrot on Tuesday doesn't mean they hate carrots forever.
  • panicking about gagging. gagging is normal and protective. it's loud and dramatic but it's the baby's way of moving food they can't swallow. choking is silent. know the difference.
